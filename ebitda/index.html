How is EBITDA Calculated?

EBITDA = Net Income + Interest + Taxes + D&A

Net Income: Final profit after all expenses/taxes.

Interest: Cost of servicing debt (Loans/Bonds).

Taxes: Corporate income tax paid to the government.

D&A: Non-cash depreciation and amortization charges.

Example EBITDA Calculation

A manufacturing firm has ₹10 Lakhs Net Profit, ₹2 Lakhs Interest, ₹3 Lakhs Taxes, and ₹5 Lakhs Depreciation:
  • Net Profit: ₹10,00,000
  • Add back: ₹2L + ₹3L + ₹5L = ₹10,00,000
  • Total EBITDA: ₹20,00,000
  • If Revenue is ₹1 Cr, EBITDA Margin is 20%

What is EBITDA and Why It Matters in 2026?

EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization) is a universal metric used to evaluate a company's operating performance. By stripping away financial and accounting decisions—such as interest rates, tax regimes, and depreciation methods—EBITDA allows investors and analysts to see the "raw" cash generation potential of a business's core operations.

In the Indian startup and corporate ecosystem of 2026, EBITDA has become the standard for valuation. While Net Profit can be skewed by heavy debt or large asset write-offs, EBITDA remains a more consistent indicator of whether a business model is truly viable. It is effectively a proxy for operating cash flow.

EBITDA vs. EBIT: The Difference

The main difference lies in Depreciation and Amortization. EBIT (Earnings Before Interest and Taxes) considers the wear and tear of physical assets (depreciation) and the usage of intangible assets (amortization) as a cost. EBITDA adds these back because they are non-cash expenses. For capital-intensive industries like manufacturing or infrastructure, the gap between EBIT and EBITDA can be massive.

How to Interpret EBITDA Margin?

EBITDA Margin shows how much profit a company generates for every Rupee of revenue. Here is a general benchmark for Indian sectors in 2026:

High Margin (20% - 40%)

Typical of Software (SaaS), Pharmaceuticals, and Specialty Chemicals. These businesses have low variable costs and high pricing power.

Focus: Scale Efficiency

Low/Modest Margin (5% - 15%)

Typical of Retail, E-commerce, and Infrastructure. These businesses rely on high volumes and high turnover to generate wealth.

Focus: Volume Growth

EBITDA Frequently Asked Questions

1. Is EBITDA the same as cash flow?
No. While EBITDA is used as a proxy for cash flow, it doesn't account for changes in working capital (like accounts receivable/payable) or capital expenditures. Real cash flow can be much higher or lower than EBITDA.
2. Why do banks use Debt/EBITDA ratios?
Lenders use this ratio to see how many years of operating profit it would take for a company to pay off its debt. A lower ratio (e.g. < 3.0) indicates a safer borrower.
3. Can EBITDA be negative?
Yes. A negative EBITDA means the company's core operating costs (COGS, salaries, rent) are higher than its revenue. This is a critical warning sign that the business model is not sustainable.
4. What is "Adjusted EBITDA"?
Startups often use Adjusted EBITDA to remove "one-time" expenses like legal fees, restructuring costs, or non-cash items like stock-based compensation (ESOPs) to show a "normalized" profit view.
5. Does EBITDA include other income?
Strictly speaking, EBITDA should only include core operating income. Non-operating income (like profit from selling a building or interest on FDs) should be excluded to assess true business health.
6. How does EBITDA help in M&A?
Acquirers use a multiple of EBITDA (e.g., 10x EBITDA) to value a company. This allows them to see the cash potential regardless of how the current owner has financed the business.
7. What is Amortization in India?
Amortization is the systematic reduction of the value of an intangible asset over its useful life, such as patents, copyrights, or the cost of acquiring software licenses.
8. Why is EBITDA criticized by Warren Buffett?
Buffett famously noted that EBITDA ignores Depreciation, which is a real cost of maintaining assets. He argued that "Depreciation is a non-cash charge, but the CapEx required to replace that machine is very real cash."
